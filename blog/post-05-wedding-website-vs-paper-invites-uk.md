# Wedding Website vs Paper Invites: Which Is Right for You?

You've just got engaged and the first big decision isn't flowers or venues — it's how to actually tell 120 people where to be and when. Paper invites feel traditional and tangible. A wedding website feels modern and convenient. But do you need both, or can one replace the other?

The honest answer: it depends on your guests, your budget, and what you want your wedding communication to feel like. Here's how to think it through.

## What Paper Invitations Do Well

There's a reason couples have been sending paper invites for generations. A beautifully printed card has a physical presence — guests pin it to their fridge, tuck it into a drawer, carry it to the post office when they're posting their RSVP. It signals formality and occasion in a way that an email link simply can't match.

Paper is also accessible. Older relatives who don't use smartphones, guests without reliable internet access, or anyone who prefers not to engage with websites will appreciate receiving something they can hold. For very formal weddings — black tie, stately homes, large family celebrations — a printed invite sets the right tone from the moment it lands on the doormat.

The downside is cost. A professionally designed and printed suite (invite, RSVP card, envelope, stamp, information card) can easily run to £4–8 per guest. For a 100-person wedding, that's £400–£800 before you've booked a single supplier.

## What a Wedding Website Does Better

A wedding website isn't just a digital invite — it's a living, updatable hub for everything your guests need to know.

Venue details with a map link. Travel and accommodation suggestions. Your gift list. Dietary preference collection. Song requests. A timeline of the day. Photos of you both. All of it in one place, accessible from any phone, updated the moment something changes.

When your accommodation block booking fills up and you need to suggest alternatives, you update the website. When the evening start time shifts by half an hour, you update the website. With paper, you'd be reprinting inserts or sending a second round of post.

RSVPs are where the difference is most stark. Paper RSVP cards get lost in the post, arrive late, or don't come back at all. Chasing guests is tedious. With an online RSVP form, responses come in automatically — you can see at a glance who's confirmed, who hasn't replied, whether anyone needs a vegetarian meal, and whether your university friends are planning to request Wonderwall. Everything collected in one place, no spreadsheet required.

## The Case for Combining Both

Most couples find the sweet spot is a hybrid approach: a physical save-the-date or formal invite, paired with a wedding website that does the heavy lifting.

**Save the date → wedding website.** Send a printed save-the-date card (often simpler and cheaper than a full invite) six to twelve months ahead. Include your wedding website URL. Guests who want to know more — venue details, accommodation options, gift ideas — can visit straight away.

**Formal invite → website for RSVPs.** When your full invitations go out, include your website address prominently alongside any paper RSVP option. Many couples find that most guests, especially younger ones, will RSVP online even when a card is available, simply because it's easier.

**Website for everything else.** Your website becomes the canonical source of truth for the entire planning period. No need for a separate information card listing hotels; no need to include a printed map. Point guests to the site and keep it updated.

This approach lets you keep the ceremony and formality of a printed invite while cutting significant cost and admin.

## What Does It Actually Cost?

Paper invitations vary enormously. A simple DIY suite printed at home costs almost nothing. Professional design and letterpress printing can cost thousands. A realistic budget for a mid-range printed invite for 80–100 guests is £400–£700 including postage.

## Which Is Right for You?

If your guests are all comfortable online and you're happy to forgo tradition, a wedding website alone works perfectly well — particularly if you pair it with a digital save-the-date.

If you're having a formal or traditional wedding, or you have many guests who'd struggle with a website, send paper invites and use a website to handle RSVPs and share detailed information.

If you want the best of both worlds — the warmth of print and the convenience of digital — combine them. A simple save-the-date card plus a well-built wedding website is the approach most UK couples settle on, and it's hard to argue with.

Whatever you decide, your guests just want to know where to be, what to wear, and whether they can bring a plus-one. Make that information easy to find and you've done the most important thing.
